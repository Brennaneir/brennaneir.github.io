An Implication of Ethical Complication

Aristotle\'s Nicomachean Ethic states that moral behaviour is between 2 extremes:

\- Excess (using all available data, without ethical concern)

\- Deficiency (not using any data at all)

Both of these extremes pose significant drawbacks, the former (excess) being unethical and risking any and all privacy of data. The latter (deficiency) being so cautious as to not gather data at all prevents any form of progress and innovation.

This is where a concept known as the ethics equilibrium comes into play, a balance between the two extremes so as to foster growth while protecting the data privacy of everyone involved.

An Ethical Conundrum

At the completion of a course, participants are given a survey of 10 questions. To perform the survey, participants access a URL in their web browser that is made available for a short period of time and requires that students log in with their username and password for the organization.

The beginning of the survey prompts for the name of the course, but not their name. Instead, the survey description on this page indicates that all answers and the survey itself will remain anonymous.

Executive management wishes to use the results of the survey to determine which courses require redesign or updating. You are the data analyst assigned to this task and are required to provide actionable insights for this purpose to executive management.

Upon reading this you may be asking where the ethical concerns are? If at all? Here is my angle on the situation:

Participants are required to login to complete the survey, while anonymity is promised, it may still be possible for executive management to determine the identity of each participant via the data provided by the participant logging in by cross-referencing the data available through the participant's logins elsewhere on the same account. This would violate the promise of privacy safety and as well open the door to prejudice and bias where perhaps the executive management would prefer to take action depending on their preferred participants rather than working with anonymous data.

What can be done from an ethical point of view

Personally I would suggest that executive management use cryptography to keep the data internal and prevent outsiders from viewing it and also that it be used in combination with pseudonymization of the user data in order to further protect the identities of the survey participants while keeping the data available in a protected form to those who plan to use it, protecting Confidentiality, Integrity, and availability simultaneously, this is known as the C.I.A triad of information security.

K anonymity which is the method of anonymizing user data by making it indistinguishable from at least one other source of data that could be used to find the identity of the user this way if a member of executive management were to unethically try to look at the identity of the participants, they would be met with an indistinguishable scramble of information that would take a far greater amount of time to identify if management was still determined enough to track it down, and this lengthened span of time may prove invaluable for ethical management to catch unethical management in the act and remove them from the company for violating company policy standards, or perhaps an unethical management member may abandon the pursuit of the participant's identity altogether which will also vastly aid in the preservation of integrity for both the management and the participants.

What if management can't see the ethical dilemma

Perhaps management does not see any issue, what is a person to do In this situation?

Let us explore what I personally would have to do about it.

First and foremost, I would take notes of all my ethical concerns to ensure I had all necessary data gathered in a reliable place, simply committing to memory is liable to fail under pressure and from there I would revisit the conversation with executive management having my notes to support my claims and reassure management that this is for the purposes of protecting the participants and is not meant in any way to be an attack on them. If management is still unwilling to listen or make any changes to the security of the survey, then from there I would take my concerns to the HR department if there is indeed an HR department and discuss the ethical actions to take to preserve the privacy of the current participants and voice suggestions changes to protect future participants' privacy. If a HR department is not available, I would instead go to any staff that is responsible for data security, keeping the issue internal as a first resort after all would seem to be the most ethical choice as often times ethics boil down to the choice that does not hurt anybody, and while sometimes that may not be entirely possible, we should explore that route before resorting to for example: exposing the unethical practices of a company to the world, painting all involved in the company as an unethical privacy violator which would be harmful indeed.

After all, has been said, and done

Being ethical may sound like a cut and clear way to operate but it is not always so simple.

Do you purge ahead as though you are a mad scientist craving results at any cost?

Do you shut down as though you are a hermit lodged up in the security of his mountain lodge yet shuttered from society, locked in a stasis of never furthering his own life?

In many aspects of life a balance is required, perhaps you sacrifice some progress in order to preserve the integrity of your endeavors, in the end we must all ask ourselves if our decisions are hurting anybody as we progress? And if our actions are indeed harmless yet still fostering our growth, a healthy balance has been achieved and after all has been said and done, we can rest easy at the end of the day with the feeling that we are making a real difference and staying ethical at the same time.
